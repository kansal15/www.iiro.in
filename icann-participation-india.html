Internet policy isn't written somewhere else

It's written where someone shows up. A practical guide to ICANN — how its policies get made, and exactly where an Indian student, researcher, or citizen can step into that process. First in a series on participating across the internet governance ecosystem.

Why it matters

Policy-making decides who gets a voice online — before the internet reaches you

The rules for domain names, IP addresses, and DNS security aren't handed down by one government or one company. They're negotiated — and whoever isn't in the room doesn't get a say.
It shapes India's digital economy directly

Decisions on new domain extensions, DNS abuse mitigation, and IP address allocation affect every Indian business, app, and citizen online.

India is under-represented relative to its user base

With one of the world's largest internet populations, India's voice in ICANN's working groups is still thin compared to its stake.

The multistakeholder model only works if stakeholders show up

Policies are shaped bottom-up by whoever participates — governments, industry, technical community, academia, and civil society, as equals.

Public policy engagement is a skill, not a credential

No law degree or government post is required — informed citizens, researchers, and students can submit comments, join working groups, and be heard.

The basics

What ICANN actually does — and who does it with

ICANN (the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) coordinates the internet's system of unique identifiers — domain names, IP addresses, and the protocols that let any device find any other device. It doesn't run the internet, but it keeps the address book consistent worldwide. Its policies are developed through a bottom-up, multistakeholder process — meaning the following groups each hold a formal seat at the table:

GNSO

Generic Names Supporting Organization — develops policy for generic top-level domains like .com and .org.

ccNSO

Country-Code Names Supporting Organization — policy for country domains, including India's own .in.

ALAC / At-Large

The formal voice of individual internet end-users, organized through Regional At-Large Organizations (RALOs) — this is where most individuals enter.

GAC

Governmental Advisory Committee — where national governments, including India's, advise the ICANN Board on public policy concerns.

SSAC & RSSAC

Security and Root Server System advisory committees — technical guardrails for DNS security and stability.

ICANN Board

Approves final policy, informed by community recommendations and GAC advice.

How a policy actually gets made

The Policy Development Process — and where India can step in

Most ICANN policy travels through a defined pipeline, not a closed-door decision. Each stage is a real, documented entry point — not a formality.

1
Issue is identified

A policy gap or emerging issue (e.g. DNS abuse, new gTLD rounds, WHOIS/registration data) is flagged by the community, staff, or the Board.

Raise emerging issues relevant to India — like multilingual (IDN) domains or DNS abuse targeting Indian users — through your RALO or ALS.
2
Working group forms

A cross-community Working Group is chartered to research the issue and draft recommendations. Membership is open — anyone can join most working groups.

Individuals and ALSes from India can join working groups directly through ICANN Join — no prior policy experience required.
3
Public Comment period

Draft recommendations are opened for public input — typically for a minimum of 40 days. Anyone, anywhere, can submit a comment.

This is the single easiest way to influence ICANN policy from India — no membership needed, just a submission.
4
Council review & GAC advice

The GNSO or ccNSO Council reviews the final recommendations. The Governmental Advisory Committee — where India's government representative sits — offers formal advice to the Board.

India's positions here are shaped by MeitY and DoT — civil society input into government consultations feeds this stage too.
5
Board adoption & implementation

The ICANN Board formally adopts the policy, and ICANN org implements it globally — becoming binding on registries and registrars everywhere, including in India.

Implementation reviews are also opened for comment — the pipeline loops back to public input.
Concrete Steps

How to actually participate — starting this month

Action 1

Submit a Public Comment

ICANN's Public Comment page lists every open proceeding — from DNS abuse mitigation to new gTLD rules. Comments are read, summarized, and formally logged as part of the policy record.

View Open Comments Action 2

Join At-Large through APRALO

India already has 12 accredited At-Large Structures (ALSes) under APRALO, the Asia-Australasia-Pacific regional organization. You can join an existing Indian ALS, or apply as an unaffiliated individual member.

Join APRALO Action 3

Learn the ropes with ICANN Learn

ICANN's own "Beginners" programme walks through how internet governance works and how policy is actually written — self-paced, free, and built for newcomers.

Start Learning Action 4

Apply for the ICANN Fellowship

A funded programme that places newcomers — including many from India — directly inside ICANN meetings, with mentorship and a path into working groups. ICANN85 was hosted in Mumbai in March 2026.

Apply Now Action 5

Track a working group's mailing list

Every RALO and working group runs an open mailing list where drafts, calls, and decisions are discussed in real time — the lowest-friction way to observe before you speak.

Spotlight

Public policy in India — where domestic engagement plugs in

ICANN engagement doesn't stand apart from India's own policy process — the two feed each other. These are the domestic channels where the same instinct to participate applies.

Ministry

MeitY Public Consultations

The Ministry of Electronics & IT regularly opens draft rules — from IT intermediary rules to data protection — for public comment, usually 30 days per the Pre-Legislative Consultation Policy.

meity.gov.in/content/public-opinion
Registry

NIXI — India's ccTLD & IXP Operator

The National Internet Exchange of India manages the .in domain and India's internet exchange points, operating under MeitY.

nixi.in
Regulator

TRAI Consultation Papers

The Telecom Regulatory Authority of India routinely publishes consultation papers on internet and telecom policy, open for stakeholder comment.

trai.gov.in
Forum

India Internet Governance Forum (IIGF)

India's national multistakeholder IGF chapter, held annually since 2021 — bringing government, industry, academia, and civil society to the same table.

indiaigf.in
Fellowship

Youth IGF India

A capacity-building fellowship for young people to research and publish on internet governance and digital policy — a strong on-ramp before ICANN or IGF participation.

youthigf.in
Portal

MyGov.in

The Government of India's citizen engagement platform, where several ministries — including MeitY — post consultations and surveys open to the public.

mygov.in
